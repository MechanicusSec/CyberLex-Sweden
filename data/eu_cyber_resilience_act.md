# EU Cyber Resilience Act

## Topic

The EU Cyber Resilience Act, cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements, and lifecycle security duties for manufacturers, importers, and distributors.

## Main legal source

The Cyber Resilience Act is Regulation (EU) 2024/2847 of the European Parliament and of the Council on horizontal cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements.

It applies to products with digital elements placed on the EU market, unless an exclusion or special sector rule applies.

## Main authority

At EU level, the Cyber Resilience Act is connected to the European Commission and EU market surveillance rules.

In Sweden, practical supervision and market surveillance may depend on national implementation and designated Swedish authorities.

CyberLex Sweden should treat the Cyber Resilience Act as an EU cybersecurity product regulation and should not guess which Swedish authority applies unless a verified Swedish source is added.

## Key idea

The Cyber Resilience Act creates common EU cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements.

The purpose is to make digital products safer before they are placed on the market and to ensure that cybersecurity is maintained during the product lifecycle.

In simple terms, manufacturers should not sell connected products or software with avoidable security weaknesses and then abandon users like tiny civilians in a hive city.

## Important points

The Cyber Resilience Act focuses on:

1. Products with digital elements

The CRA applies to many products that contain software or connect directly or indirectly to networks or other devices.

Examples can include:

- connected devices
- software products
- apps
- embedded systems
- smart devices
- routers
- connected cameras
- industrial digital products
- security-relevant software components

2. Cybersecurity by design and by default

Manufacturers must consider cybersecurity during design, development, production, delivery, and maintenance.

Security should not be added as an afterthought.

3. Vulnerability handling

Manufacturers must handle vulnerabilities during the support period of the product.

This includes identifying, documenting, fixing, and communicating about vulnerabilities where required.

4. Security updates

Products should be supported with appropriate security updates during the expected product support period.

5. Incident and vulnerability reporting

The CRA includes reporting duties for actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents affecting product security.

6. Documentation and conformity

Manufacturers must prepare technical documentation and follow conformity assessment rules before placing products on the EU market.

7. Responsibilities across the supply chain

The CRA includes duties for manufacturers, authorised representatives, importers, and distributors.

## Products with digital elements explained

A product with digital elements is generally a hardware or software product whose intended or foreseeable use includes a direct or indirect logical or physical data connection to a device or network.

In practical cybersecurity language, this means the CRA can be relevant when a product:

- contains software
- connects to the internet
- connects to a local network
- communicates with another device
- receives remote updates
- sends or receives data
- depends on digital components
- has a cybersecurity impact if compromised

The exact legal scope must always be checked against the regulation and official guidance.

## Cybersecurity by design

Cybersecurity by design means cybersecurity should be built into the product from the beginning.

CyberLex Sweden should explain this as:

- secure development practices
- secure default configuration
- minimising attack surfaces
- access control
- secure authentication
- encryption where appropriate
- protection against unauthorised access
- logging and monitoring where relevant
- secure update mechanisms
- vulnerability management
- protection against known exploitable weaknesses

A product should not rely only on the customer fixing security problems after purchase.

## Vulnerability handling

The CRA requires manufacturers to think about vulnerability handling throughout the product lifecycle.

Important practical questions include:

- Does the manufacturer have a vulnerability handling process?
- Can security researchers report vulnerabilities?
- Are vulnerabilities assessed and prioritised?
- Are fixes developed and released?
- Are users informed when action is needed?
- Are security updates delivered securely?
- Is the support period clear?
- Is vulnerability information documented?

This is relevant for secure software development, product security, supply chain security, and responsible disclosure.

## Security updates

Security updates are an important part of CRA compliance.

CyberLex Sweden should explain that products with digital elements may need security updates during their support period.

Relevant questions include:

- How long will the product receive security updates?
- Are updates automatic or manual?
- Are update mechanisms protected against tampering?
- Are users informed about updates?
- Are updates provided without unnecessary delay?
- Can users understand whether the product is still supported?

Unsupported products can create cybersecurity risk for users, companies, and society.

## CRA assessment checklist

When assessing whether the Cyber Resilience Act may be relevant, CyberLex Sweden should consider:

- Is the question about a product, software, app, connected device, or digital component?
- Is the product placed on the EU market?
- Does the product connect directly or indirectly to a device or network?
- Could a cybersecurity weakness affect users, systems, or data?
- Is the actor a manufacturer, importer, distributor, or other supply-chain actor?
- Does the issue involve secure design, secure defaults, or product hardening?
- Does the issue involve vulnerability handling or responsible disclosure?
- Does the issue involve security updates or end-of-support?
- Does the issue involve an actively exploited vulnerability?
- Does the issue involve a severe incident affecting product security?
- Is another EU law more specific for the product or sector?

This checklist is only for educational assessment. It is not legal advice.

## Relationship with NIS2, GDPR, and DORA

The Cyber Resilience Act can overlap with other cybersecurity and data protection rules.

CRA focuses on cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements.

NIS2 focuses on cybersecurity risk management and incident reporting for essential and important entities.

GDPR focuses on personal data protection and personal data breach notification.

DORA focuses on digital operational resilience in the financial sector.

Example:

A vulnerable connected product used by a financial company may raise several questions:

- CRA questions about product security, vulnerability handling, and manufacturer duties
- DORA questions if the product affects financial-sector digital operational resilience
- NIS2 questions if the organization is covered by NIS2-related cybersecurity duties
- GDPR questions if personal data is exposed or compromised

The same technical incident can therefore create different legal questions under different legal frameworks.

## Cybersecurity connection

The Cyber Resilience Act is directly connected to cybersecurity because insecure products can create large-scale risks.

Weak product security can lead to:

- unauthorized access
- botnets
- ransomware entry points
- data breaches
- insecure IoT devices
- supply chain attacks
- exploitation of known vulnerabilities
- unsafe default passwords
- abandoned products without patches
- compromised software updates

The CRA tries to reduce these risks by making cybersecurity a product responsibility before and after the product reaches the market.

## Swedish summary

Cyber Resilience Act är EU:s förordning om cybersäkerhetskrav för produkter med digitala element.

Den gäller många typer av mjukvara, uppkopplade produkter och digitala komponenter som släpps ut på EU-marknaden.

Syftet är att produkter ska vara säkrare från början och att tillverkare ska ta ansvar för cybersäkerhet under produktens livscykel.

Viktiga delar i CRA är:

- cybersäkerhet genom design och standardinställningar
- säkrare produkter med digitala element
- hantering av sårbarheter
- säkerhetsuppdateringar
- teknisk dokumentation
- krav på tillverkare, importörer och distributörer
- rapportering av vissa aktivt utnyttjade sårbarheter och allvarliga incidenter

CRA är relevant när en fråga handlar om mjukvara, appar, uppkopplade enheter, IoT, digitala produkter, säkerhetsuppdateringar, sårbarheter eller produktansvar för cybersäkerhet.
