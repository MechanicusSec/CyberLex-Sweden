# EU attacks against information systems

## Topic

Directive 2013/40/EU on attacks against information systems, EU cybercrime rules, illegal access, illegal system interference, illegal data interference, and misuse of cyber tools.

## Main legal source

The main EU legal source is Directive 2013/40/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of 12 August 2013 on attacks against information systems.

The directive replaced Council Framework Decision 2005/222/JHA.

The directive sets minimum rules for criminal offences and sanctions concerning attacks against information systems.

## Main authority or legal source

At EU level, the key legal source is Directive 2013/40/EU.

The directive is connected to EU criminal-law cooperation and cooperation between competent authorities, including police, judicial authorities, Europol, Eurojust, and other relevant EU bodies.

In Sweden, the directive is relevant as background EU law for cybercrime rules, but CyberLex Sweden should use Swedish criminal-law sources for direct Swedish criminal-law answers.

## Key idea

Directive 2013/40/EU aims to harmonise EU Member States’ criminal law on attacks against information systems.

The directive creates minimum rules for offences such as illegal access, illegal system interference, illegal data interference, illegal interception, and misuse of tools.

The purpose is to make sure Member States criminalise serious cyberattacks and cooperate more effectively against cybercrime.

## Important points

Directive 2013/40/EU covers several core cybercrime concepts:

1. Illegal access to information systems

This concerns intentional access to an information system without right.

2. Illegal system interference

This concerns serious hindering or interruption of the functioning of an information system.

Examples can include attacks that disrupt availability, such as denial-of-service or distributed denial-of-service attacks.

3. Illegal data interference

This concerns deleting, damaging, deteriorating, altering, suppressing, or rendering computer data inaccessible without right.

4. Illegal interception

This concerns intercepting non-public transmissions of computer data without right.

5. Misuse of tools

This concerns certain tools, access credentials, passwords, or computer programs used for committing offences, when the legal conditions are met.

6. Aggravating circumstances

The directive also addresses more serious cases, including attacks using botnets, causing serious damage, or targeting critical infrastructure.

## Illegal access explained

Illegal access means gaining access to an information system without permission or legal right.

In practical cybersecurity terms, this can include:

- logging into an account without permission
- exploiting a vulnerability to enter a system
- bypassing authentication
- using stolen credentials
- accessing servers, databases, or admin panels without authorisation
- exceeding the permission granted in a testing or work context

CyberLex Sweden should explain that permission and scope are central.

Security testing can be lawful when it is authorised and stays within the agreed scope. The same technical action can become legally risky if it is done without authorisation or outside the agreed scope.

## Illegal system interference explained

Illegal system interference focuses on attacks against the functioning of an information system.

Examples can include:

- denial-of-service attacks
- distributed denial-of-service attacks
- disrupting servers or services
- overloading systems
- preventing legitimate users from accessing services
- interfering with normal system operation

This is relevant for cyberattacks where the main harm is disruption, downtime, or loss of availability.

## Illegal data interference explained

Illegal data interference focuses on attacks against computer data.

Examples can include:

- deleting data
- altering data
- corrupting files
- making data inaccessible
- encrypting data without authorisation
- damaging databases
- manipulating logs or records

Ransomware can be relevant to this type of concept because it may make data inaccessible and disrupt systems.

## Misuse of tools explained

The directive includes rules about misuse of tools under certain legal conditions.

In cybersecurity terms, this can involve:

- malware
- exploit tools
- botnet tools
- stolen passwords
- access codes
- credentials
- software designed or adapted for committing cyber offences

CyberLex Sweden should explain this carefully.

Many tools can have both legitimate and illegitimate uses. For example, penetration testing tools can be used for lawful security testing, but they can also be misused for unauthorised attacks.

The legal assessment depends on authorisation, intent, context, and how the tool is used.

## Botnets and DDoS

Directive 2013/40/EU is especially relevant for botnets and large-scale attacks.

A botnet is a network of compromised devices controlled by an attacker.

Botnets can be used for:

- distributed denial-of-service attacks
- credential attacks
- spam
- malware distribution
- large-scale intrusion attempts
- coordinated disruption

The directive treats certain large-scale or serious attacks as more severe.
