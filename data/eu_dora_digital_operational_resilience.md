# DORA and digital operational resilience

## Topic

DORA, the Digital Operational Resilience Act, and cybersecurity resilience duties for financial entities in the European Union.

## Main legal source

DORA is Regulation (EU) 2022/2554 of the European Parliament and of the Council on digital operational resilience for the financial sector.

DORA applies across the European Union and is directly relevant in Sweden for financial entities and certain ICT third-party service providers connected to the financial sector.

## Main authority

At EU level, DORA is connected to the European Commission and the European Supervisory Authorities:

- European Banking Authority, EBA
- European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority, EIOPA
- European Securities and Markets Authority, ESMA

In Sweden, supervision depends on the type of financial entity. Finansinspektionen is the main Swedish financial supervisory authority for many financial-sector actors.

## Key idea

DORA creates a common EU framework for digital operational resilience in the financial sector.

The purpose is not only to prevent cyber incidents, but also to make sure financial entities can withstand, respond to, recover from, and learn from ICT-related disruptions.

DORA focuses on the ability of financial entities to continue operating even when they face cyberattacks, system failures, outages, third-party failures, or other ICT-related incidents.

## Important points

DORA covers several major areas:

1. ICT risk management

Financial entities must manage ICT risks as part of their governance and risk management. This includes identifying, protecting, detecting, responding to, and recovering from ICT-related threats and weaknesses.

2. ICT-related incident management and reporting

Financial entities must have processes to detect, classify, handle, document, and report major ICT-related incidents.

3. Digital operational resilience testing

Financial entities must test their digital operational resilience. Depending on size, risk, and role, this can include vulnerability assessments, scenario testing, penetration testing, and more advanced threat-led penetration testing.

4. ICT third-party risk management

DORA places strong focus on risks from ICT service providers, including cloud providers, outsourcing providers, software providers, data services, and other external technology suppliers.

5. Information sharing

DORA allows financial entities to share cyber threat information and intelligence under certain conditions, to improve collective resilience.

## Digital operational resilience explained

Digital operational resilience means the ability of a financial entity to build, assure, and review its operational integrity and reliability.

In practical cybersecurity terms, this means the organization should be able to:

- keep critical services running during ICT disruptions
- detect cyber incidents and system failures
- respond to attacks and outages
- recover systems and services
- learn from incidents
- manage risks from suppliers and outsourced ICT services
- test whether security and continuity measures actually work

DORA is therefore broader than ordinary IT security. It connects cybersecurity, business continuity, incident response, governance, risk management, supplier management, and testing.

## ICT risk management

Under DORA, ICT risk management is a core responsibility.

A financial entity should understand:

- which ICT systems support critical or important functions
- which threats could affect those systems
- which vulnerabilities exist
- which suppliers and third parties are involved
- which controls reduce the risk
- how the organization detects incidents
- how the organization restores services after disruptions

Examples of relevant controls include:

- access control
- logging and monitoring
- backup and recovery
- network security
- vulnerability management
- incident response procedures
- business continuity planning
- disaster recovery testing
- supplier risk management
- staff training and awareness

## ICT incident reporting

DORA requires financial entities to manage ICT-related incidents in a structured way.

A financial entity should normally assess:

- what happened
- when the incident started
- which systems were affected
- whether critical or important functions were affected
- whether customers or counterparties were affected
- whether data was lost, altered, unavailable, or exposed
- whether a third-party ICT provider was involved
- what containment and recovery steps were taken
- whether the incident may need to be reported

DORA incident reporting is separate from GDPR personal data breach notification, but the same incident may sometimes trigger both DORA and GDPR duties.

For example, a ransomware attack against a bank could be relevant under DORA because it affects operational resilience. If personal data is also compromised, GDPR breach assessment may also be needed.

## Third-party ICT risk

DORA gives special attention to ICT third-party risk.

Financial entities often rely on external providers for:

- cloud services
- software platforms
- payment infrastructure
- data centres
- cybersecurity services
- managed IT services
- communication services
- analytics and reporting tools

DORA requires financial entities to understand and manage these dependencies.

Important practical questions include:

- Which ICT providers support critical or important functions?
- Is there a written contract?
- Are security requirements clear?
- Are incident notification duties clear?
- Are exit and termination plans documented?
- Can the financial entity continue operating if the provider fails?
- Is concentration risk present because many services depend on one provider?

## Resilience testing

DORA requires testing of digital operational resilience.

Testing can include:

- vulnerability assessments
- security assessments
- scenario-based testing
- business continuity testing
- backup and recovery testing
- incident response exercises
- penetration testing
- threat-led penetration testing for certain entities

The purpose is to check whether policies and controls work in practice.

A written policy is not enough if the organization cannot detect, respond to, or recover from real incidents.

## DORA assessment checklist

When assessing whether DORA may be relevant, CyberLex Sweden should consider:

- Is the organization part of the financial sector?
- Is the organization a financial entity covered by DORA?
- Does the issue involve ICT systems, cyber incidents, outages, or digital services?
- Does the incident affect critical or important functions?
- Is an ICT third-party provider involved?
- Is there a major ICT-related incident?
- Are customers, counterparties, or services affected?
- Are logs, timelines, and technical evidence preserved?
- Has the organization assessed whether reporting is required?
- Could the same incident also involve GDPR personal data breach rules?
- Could NIS2 or other cybersecurity rules also be relevant?

This checklist is only for educational assessment. It is not legal advice.

## Relationship with NIS2 and GDPR

DORA, NIS2, and GDPR can overlap, but they focus on different things.

DORA focuses on digital operational resilience in the financial sector.

NIS2 focuses on cybersecurity risk management and incident reporting for essential and important entities across many sectors.

GDPR focuses on protection of personal data and personal data breach notification.

A single cyber incident can therefore create several separate legal questions.

Example:

A ransomware attack against a financial entity may require:

- DORA assessment if ICT systems or financial services are affected
- GDPR assessment if personal data is compromised
- NIS2 assessment if the entity is also covered by NIS2-related cybersecurity rules
- internal documentation, evidence preservation, containment, and recovery actions

## Cybersecurity connection

DORA is highly relevant for cybersecurity because modern financial services depend on digital systems.

Cybersecurity incidents can affect:

- payment services
- banking operations
- insurance systems
- trading platforms
- customer portals
- identity and access systems
- cloud services
- outsourced IT operations
- transaction monitoring
- financial reporting

DORA treats cybersecurity as part of operational resilience. The legal issue is not only whether an attack happened, but whether the organization was prepared, could respond, could recover, and could manage supplier risk.

## Swedish summary

DORA är EU:s förordning om digital operativ motståndskraft för finanssektorn.

DORA handlar om att finansiella aktörer ska kunna hantera IT-risker, cyberincidenter, driftstörningar, leverantörsrisker och återställning av viktiga digitala tjänster.

DORA är inte bara en cybersäkerhetsregel. Den handlar också om kontinuitet, styrning, riskhantering, incidenthantering, testning och beroenden av externa ICT-leverantörer.

Viktiga delar i DORA är:

- hantering av ICT-risker
- rapportering och hantering av ICT-relaterade incidenter
- testning av digital operativ motståndskraft
- hantering av risker från ICT-tredjepartsleverantörer
- informationsdelning om cyberhot

En cyberattack mot en bank, ett försäkringsbolag eller en annan finansiell aktör kan därför behöva bedömas enligt DORA. Om personuppgifter påverkas kan även GDPR bli relevant.
