# Wrong Email Customer Data Case

## Case type

Authority decision / GDPR security measures / personal data breach / accidental disclosure by email.

## Jurisdiction

Sweden.

## Year

2023.

## Authority or court

Integritetsskyddsmyndigheten (IMY), the Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection.

## Topic

Wrong email attachment, customer data, financial information, personal data breach, Article 32 GDPR, human error, technical and organisational security measures.

## Short summary

IMY issued an administrative fine against Indecap after the company accidentally sent an email with an Excel attachment containing personal data about thousands of other customers.

The attachment contained report material with personal data about more than 52,000 customers.

The information included names, personal identity numbers, bank, email address, individual fund choices, and the latest imported value of customers' holdings in those funds.

The file did not include account numbers or login credentials, but it still contained financial and identity-related customer information.

The case is important because it shows that ordinary office work can create serious GDPR risk when personal data is handled without strong enough controls.

This was not a hacking case, ransomware incident, or external attack.

Instead, the breach happened through a common operational mistake: the wrong attachment was sent by email.

The case shows that human error is not automatically excused under GDPR if the organization has not reduced foreseeable risks with appropriate technical and organisational measures.

It also shows why email routines, attachment checks, approval workflows, access limits, and data minimisation matter when large customer datasets are handled.

The case is useful as an example of how accidental disclosure can become a personal data breach and lead to an administrative fine.

## What happened

Indecap sent an email to a large number of customers.

The email accidentally included an Excel document containing report material with personal data about other customers.

The file included information such as names, personal identity numbers, bank, email address, individual fund choice, and the latest imported value of the customer's holdings in those funds.

The file did not include account numbers or login credentials, but it contained personal data about more than 52,000 customers.

Because the attachment was sent to the wrong recipients, personal data was disclosed to people who should not have received it.

The incident shows how a single incorrect attachment can expose a very large amount of customer data.

It also shows that the risk is not limited to whether the most sensitive data, such as passwords or account numbers, was included.

Financial choices, customer identity information, and investment-related information can still create privacy risks when disclosed to unauthorized recipients.

The case is a clear example of how manual email handling can become a weak point in data protection.

## Legal issue

The central legal issue was whether the company had ensured a suitable level of security for the personal data processing.

The case shows that human error can still create GDPR risk if technical and organisational measures are not sufficient to reduce the risk of wrong attachments, wrong recipients, or accidental disclosure.

The case is relevant to Article 32 GDPR, email security, access control, data minimisation, attachment handling, approval workflows, and secure routines for sending customer information.

It also raises the question of whether large customer datasets should be attached to ordinary emails at all.

## Decision or outcome

IMY found that the company had processed personal data in breach of the GDPR by not ensuring an appropriate level of security in relation to the risks.

IMY issued an administrative fine of SEK 500,000 against Indecap.

The decision shows that an accidental email mistake can still become a GDPR infringement when the surrounding controls are not strong enough.

It also shows that organizations must consider predictable human mistakes when designing security measures.

The case is useful because it connects everyday administrative work to Article 32 GDPR security obligations.

## Why it matters for CyberLex

This case is useful because it shows that not every personal data breach comes from hacking.

Ordinary operational mistakes, such as sending the wrong attachment, can also become GDPR incidents.

The case is especially relevant because it involved a large number of customers and financial customer information.

It shows that organizations should not rely only on employees being careful when sending personal data.

A personal data breach can happen when:

* the wrong attachment is added to an email
* a large customer file is sent without enough checks
* personal data is exported into spreadsheets without clear controls
* approval routines are missing or too weak
* email is used for information that should have been shared through a safer channel
* access to large datasets is broader than necessary
* data minimisation is not applied before sending information

The case is relevant for questions about email security, accidental disclosure, customer data, financial data, human error, internal routines, approval workflows, and technical controls that reduce the risk of sending the wrong information.

## Learning note

This case shows that personal data breaches do not always involve hackers.

Human error, weak email routines, spreadsheet exports, missing approval workflows, and missing technical controls can expose large amounts of customer data and lead to GDPR consequences.

Organizations should design processes that assume mistakes will happen and reduce the impact before the mistake reaches customers.

Useful safeguards can include attachment checks, second-person review, restricted access to exported datasets, safer sharing platforms, recipient verification, warning labels for large files, and data minimisation before sending.

The painfully ordinary lesson is that “I attached the wrong file” can become a regulatory incident when the file contains thousands of people’s personal data.
