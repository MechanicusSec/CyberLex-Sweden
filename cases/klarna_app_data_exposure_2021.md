# Klarna App Data Exposure 2021

## Case type

Public incident / supervisory investigation / app data exposure / possible personal data breach.

## Jurisdiction

Sweden.

## Year

2021.

## Authority or court

Klarna Bank AB.

Finansinspektionen, the Swedish Financial Supervisory Authority, opened an investigation into the incident.

No CyberLex source currently confirms an IMY administrative fine for this specific app data exposure incident.

## Topic

Klarna, app data exposure, customer data, financial-sector data, technical error, human error, app update, confidentiality, possible personal data breach, incident response.

## Short summary

In May 2021, Klarna experienced an app incident where some users could see information connected to other users' accounts for a limited time.

Public reporting described the incident as caused by human error during an app update. Klarna reportedly stated that the issue lasted about 31 minutes and affected up to about 9,500 users.

Finansinspektionen later announced that it had opened an investigation into whether the incident violated banking confidentiality rules.

This case is useful for CyberLex Sweden because it shows that personal data exposure can happen through technical errors in apps, not only through hacking, malware, ransomware, or deliberate attacks.

## What happened

On 27 May 2021, Klarna experienced an IT incident connected to its app.

During the incident, some customers were reportedly able to access information about other customers.

Public reporting stated that the issue was caused by human error during an app update and that the incident lasted about 31 minutes.

Klarna reportedly said that up to about 9,500 users were affected and that card and bank details were not shown.

Earlier media reports mentioned larger numbers, but later reporting stated that the lower figure was Klarna's own description of the affected user group.

Finansinspektionen announced in July 2021 that it had opened an investigation into the incident and whether it violated the duty of confidentiality in banking operations.

## Swedish what happened

Klarna uppgavs ha sagt att upp till cirka 9 500 användare berördes och att kort- och bankuppgifter inte visades.

Tidigare medierapportering nämnde högre antal, men senare rapportering angav den lägre siffran som Klarnas egen beskrivning av den berörda användargruppen.

Finansinspektionen meddelade i juli 2021 att myndigheten hade inlett en undersökning av incidenten och om den innebar ett brott mot tystnadsplikten i bankverksamhet.

## Why it matters for CyberLex

This case is important for CyberLex Sweden because it shows that personal data exposure can happen through ordinary technical mistakes in digital services.

The incident was not mainly described as an external hacking attack. It was connected to an app issue where users could see information connected to other users.

This helps CyberLex explain that data exposure can happen through:

* app update errors
* session or account-mapping mistakes
* weak release testing
* insufficient technical safeguards
* incorrect user-data separation
* unclear incident communication
* fast-moving deployment changes

The case is especially useful because Klarna operates in the financial sector, where customer confidentiality and trust are central.

## Swedish why it matters for CyberLex

Incidenten beskrevs inte främst som en extern hackerattack. Den var kopplad till ett appfel där användare kunde se information kopplad till andra användare.

Detta hjälper CyberLex att förklara att dataexponering kan ske genom:

* fel vid appuppdateringar
* misstag i sessioner eller kontokopplingar
* otillräcklig testning före release
* otillräckliga tekniska skyddsåtgärder
* felaktig separering av användardata
* otydlig incidentkommunikation
* snabba produktionsändringar

Fallet är särskilt användbart eftersom Klarna verkar i den finansiella sektorn, där kundsekretess och förtroende är centrala.

## Learning note

This case shows that even a short technical app incident can create serious privacy, confidentiality, and trust risks. Secure deployment, testing, account separation, and incident communication matter just as much as protection against external attackers.
