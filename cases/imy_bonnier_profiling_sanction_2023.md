# Bonnier News Profiling Sanction

## Case type

IMY decision / GDPR profiling / marketing / cookies / lawful basis / consent / administrative fine.

## Jurisdiction

Sweden.

## Year

2023.

## Authority or court

Integritetsskyddsmyndigheten (IMY), the Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection.

The decision was later reviewed by the Administrative Court in Stockholm, which upheld IMY's sanction in February 2025.

## Topic

Bonnier News, profiling, marketing, cookies, web behaviour, customer data, consent, legitimate interest, lawful basis, GDPR, administrative fine.

## Short summary

IMY issued an administrative fine of SEK 13 million against Bonnier News AB after finding that the company had profiled customers and web visitors without valid consent.

The case concerned how Bonnier News collected and combined personal data from several sources for marketing purposes.

The data included information about purchases made in different companies within the group and browsing behaviour on Bonnier websites.

In some cases, this data was combined with externally purchased personal data, such as gender, household car ownership, postal code, and statistical information about the person's residential area.

Bonnier News had relied on legitimate interest as the legal basis for the processing.

IMY assessed that this type of extensive profiling for marketing purposes required consent.

The case is important because it shows that marketing and profiling can create serious GDPR risk even when there is no data breach, cyberattack, or security incident.

It also shows that web behaviour and customer data cannot automatically be reused and combined for targeted marketing just because the organization already has access to the data.

The case adds a useful non-incident example to CyberLex Sweden's case library.

It is especially relevant for questions about cookies, profiling, targeted advertising, lawful basis, consent, and customer-data use.

## What happened

IMY reviewed how Bonnier News collected and processed personal data for marketing purposes.

The company collected personal data from several sources.

The data was used for targeted online advertising, physical direct marketing, and telephone sales.

The processing included information about purchases made in different companies within the Bonnier group.

It also included browsing behaviour, meaning information about how internet users had used Bonnier websites.

In some cases, the data was combined with other personal data bought from external sources.

Examples included gender, household car ownership, postal code, and statistical data based on the person's residential area, such as life phase, purchasing power, and housing type.

Bonnier News argued that the processing could be based on legitimate interest.

IMY assessed that customers and web visitors could not reasonably expect such extensive profiling without first giving consent.

The case was handled through cooperation with other EU data protection authorities because Bonnier had users in several countries.

## Legal issue

The central legal issue was whether Bonnier News could rely on legitimate interest for extensive profiling of customers and web visitors for marketing purposes.

IMY assessed that the processing required consent.

The case is relevant to GDPR lawful basis, consent, legitimate interest, profiling, cookies, behavioural data, marketing, customer databases, and transparency.

It also raises the question of what users can reasonably expect when they visit websites or buy services from companies within the same group.

## Decision or outcome

IMY issued an administrative fine of SEK 13 million against Bonnier News AB.

IMY found that Bonnier News had profiled customers and web visitors without consent.

IMY assessed that legitimate interest could not be used as the legal basis for this type of extensive profiling.

The Administrative Court in Stockholm later upheld IMY's sanction in February 2025.

According to IMY's case page, Bonnier News appealed the Administrative Court's judgment to the Administrative Court of Appeal.

The case should therefore be described carefully as an IMY sanction that has been upheld at first court level, while further appeal status may need to be checked in official sources.

## Swedish decision or outcome

## Fine or cost

Administrative fine or outcome:

Bonnier News AB: SEK 13,000,000.

CyberLex should not present this amount as a fine prediction for other cases.

The amount is a historical example from one IMY decision.

Fine levels depend on the facts of each case, including the type of personal data, the scale of processing, legal basis, transparency, user expectations, safeguards, cooperation with the authority, mitigation, and other GDPR factors.

The case also involved a separate reprimand for another Bonnier group company concerning insufficient information about legal basis.

## Why it matters for CyberLex

This case is important because it adds a GDPR enforcement example that is not about hacking, ransomware, accidental disclosure, or technical security failure.

It shows that ordinary marketing and data-use practices can still create major GDPR risk.

A company may have access to customer data, web behaviour, cookies, purchase history, and external data sources, but that does not automatically mean it can combine those data sources for profiling.

The case is useful when explaining the difference between legitimate interest and consent.

It also shows why user expectations matter when assessing whether a legal basis is appropriate.

The case is useful for questions about:

* whether profiling requires consent
* whether web behaviour can be used for marketing
* whether customer data can be combined across companies
* whether external data sources can be merged into customer profiles
* whether legitimate interest is enough for targeted advertising
* how GDPR applies outside incident response and cybersecurity breaches

## Learning note

This case shows that GDPR enforcement is not only about data breaches and security incidents.

Profiling customers and web visitors for marketing can require consent, especially when browsing behaviour, purchase history, and external data sources are combined.

The case also shows that legitimate interest is not a magic spell that makes every marketing database lawful.

Organizations should assess what data is collected, why it is collected, whether users would reasonably expect the processing, whether consent is required, and whether the processing is clearly explained.

The painfully commercial lesson is that “we already have the data” is not the same as “we may use it for whatever marketing machine we built.”

## Swedish learning note

Fallet visar att GDPR-tillsyn inte bara handlar om personuppgiftsincidenter och säkerhetsbrister.

Profilering av kunder och webbesökare för marknadsföring kan kräva samtycke, särskilt när surfbeteende, köphistorik och externa datakällor kombineras.

Fallet visar också att intresseavvägning inte är en magisk formel som gör varje marknadsföringsdatabas laglig.

Organisationer bör bedöma vilka uppgifter som samlas in, varför de samlas in, om användarna rimligen kan förvänta sig behandlingen, om samtycke krävs och om behandlingen förklaras tydligt.

Den plågsamt kommersiella lärdomen är att “vi har redan uppgifterna” inte är samma sak som “vi får använda dem till vilken marknadsföringsmaskin vi än byggt.”
