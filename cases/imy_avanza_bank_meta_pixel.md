# Avanza Bank and Meta Pixel

## Case type

IMY decision / EDPB summary

## Jurisdiction

Sweden / EU

## Year

2024

## Authority or court

IMY, Integritetsskyddsmyndigheten

European Data Protection Board, EDPB

## Topic

GDPR, personal data transfer, Meta Pixel, customer data, technical and organisational measures, financial-sector data, administrative fine.

## Short summary

IMY issued an administrative fine of SEK 15 million against Avanza Bank AB after personal data from up to one million customers was transferred to Meta through the bank's use of Meta Pixel.

The issue was connected to Avanza's website and app, where tracking technology was used in a way that resulted in customer data being sent to Meta.

According to the EDPB summary, the incorrect transfer occurred between 15 November 2019 and 2 June 2021.

The case is important because it involved a bank, which means the affected data was connected to a financial-sector environment where customers expect a high level of confidentiality and control.

The incident was not described as a traditional cyberattack where an external attacker broke into the bank's systems.

Instead, the GDPR risk came from a third-party tracking tool that created an uncontrolled or incorrect data flow to an external platform.

This makes the case useful as an example of how ordinary website and app integrations can become personal data incidents.

It also shows that organizations need to understand what tracking tools collect, where the data is sent, and whether personal data may be exposed to third parties.

Avanza later deactivated the pixel and developed internal routines after becoming aware of the incorrect transfer.

The case shows that technical and organisational measures are not only about firewalls, passwords, or access control, but also about reviewing scripts, pixels, analytics tools, and third-party integrations before they are deployed.

## What happened

Avanza Bank used Meta Pixel on its website and in its app.

According to the EDPB summary, Avanza's notification stated that personal data of up to one million customers was wrongly transferred to Meta between 15 November 2019 and 2 June 2021.

IMY stated that the incorrect transfer concerned customer data and that the bank had later deactivated the pixel after becoming aware of what had happened.

The case was connected to the use, handling, or configuration of tracking technology in Avanza's digital services.

The incident is especially important because it shows that personal data can leave an organization through an integration even when there is no hacking, malware, or direct system compromise.

Website and app tracking can create hidden data flows that are difficult for users to see and sometimes difficult for organizations to fully understand without careful review.

In a banking context, this kind of data flow can be especially sensitive because customers may reasonably expect strict control over information connected to their relationship with the bank.

IMY also stated that Avanza had developed internal routines after discovering the incorrect transfer.

## Decision or outcome

IMY issued an administrative fine of SEK 15 million against Avanza Bank AB.

The case shows that tracking technology can become a GDPR problem when it causes personal data to be transferred to a third party in an uncontrolled or unlawful way.

It also shows that organizations are expected to understand and control how third-party scripts and pixels process personal data.

The case is especially useful because the transfer happened through ordinary digital service functionality, not through a classic external attack.

## Swedish decision or outcome

Fallet visar att spårningsteknik kan bli ett GDPR-problem när tekniken leder till att personuppgifter överförs till tredje part på ett okontrollerat eller otillåtet sätt.

Det visar också att organisationer förväntas förstå och kontrollera hur tredjepartsskript och pixlar behandlar personuppgifter.

Fallet är särskilt användbart eftersom överföringen skedde genom vanlig digital tjänstefunktionalitet, inte genom en klassisk extern attack.

This case is important because it shows that data leaks or GDPR problems do not only happen through hacking or ransomware.

A privacy or security incident can also happen through:

* misconfigured tracking tools

* website or app integrations
* external platforms receiving personal data
* weak internal review before deployment
* unclear responsibility for third-party scripts
* poor documentation of data flows
* lack of testing before publishing tracking technology

The case is also useful because it involves the financial sector, where confidentiality and trust are especially important.

It helps explain that technical and organisational measures under GDPR include reviewing how digital services are built, not only how servers and user accounts are protected.

The case can be used when answering questions about tracking tools, accidental data transfers, internal routines, and the risks of sending personal data to external platforms.

## Learning note

This case shows that financial-sector data and tracking technology require strong control before deployment.

A misconfigured or poorly reviewed tracking tool can become a data protection incident even when there is no traditional cyberattack.

The case also shows that organizations need to map and understand data flows created by third-party tools.

Before using tracking pixels, analytics scripts, or marketing integrations, organizations should assess what data may be collected, whether personal data may be transferred, which third parties receive the data, and what technical and organisational measures are needed.

Fallet visar också att organisationer behöver kartlägga och förstå de dataflöden som skapas av tredjepartsverktyg.
