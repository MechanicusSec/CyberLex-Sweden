# IMY Kry Meta Pixel

## Case type

Authority decision / GDPR security measures / Meta Pixel / tracking technology / personal data breach.

## Jurisdiction

Sweden.

## Year

2024.

## Authority or court

Integritetsskyddsmyndigheten (IMY), the Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection.

## Topic

Kry, Meta Pixel, Automatic Advanced Matching, hashed contact information, third-party tracking, Article 32 GDPR, healthcare context, personal data breach, reprimand.

## Short summary

IMY found that Kry International AB processed personal data in breach of Article 32(1) GDPR by not implementing all appropriate technical and organisational measures when using Meta Pixel.

The case concerned the unintended transfer of hashed contact information to Meta through Meta Pixel and its Automatic Advanced Matching function.

Kry used Meta Pixel on websites connected to a service where users could invite organisations, colleagues, customers, patients, or representatives of patients to video meetings through links sent by text message or email.

Because of the pixel configuration and Automatic Advanced Matching, hashed contact information about end-users was unintentionally transferred to Meta.

The transfer started on 28 May 2020 and ended on 17 May 2022, and Kry notified IMY about the incident as a personal data breach in May 2022.

This case is important because it shows that hashed data can still create GDPR risk when it relates to identifiable people or can be linked to other information.

It also shows that tracking technology can create hidden or unintended personal data flows, even when an organisation does not intend to share the data.

The case is more nuanced than the Meta Pixel cases that ended with large administrative fines, because IMY issued a reprimand instead of a fine.

That makes the decision useful for understanding how GDPR outcomes depend on the facts of the case, the type of data, the risk level, the safeguards, the organisation's actions, and the authority's overall assessment.

The case also highlights that healthcare-related contexts require careful technical review, testing, monitoring, and documentation before tracking tools or third-party scripts are used.

## What happened

Kry used Meta Pixel on websites connected to a service that allowed users to invite organisations, colleagues, customers, patients, or representatives of patients to video meetings through links sent by text message or email.

The websites were connected to a healthcare-related digital service environment, which made the processing context important from a risk perspective.

Because of Meta Pixel and the Automatic Advanced Matching function, hashed contact information about end-users was unintentionally transferred to Meta.

Automatic Advanced Matching can use information such as contact details to help match website or service activity with users on Meta's platforms.

In this case, the issue was that the function caused contact information to be transferred in a way that Kry had not intended.

The incident was notified to IMY as a personal data breach in May 2022.

The transmission started on 28 May 2020 and ended on 17 May 2022.

The case shows that an organisation may create a personal data breach through the configuration or behaviour of a third-party tool, even without a traditional cyberattack, malware infection, or external intrusion.

It also shows that tracking tools must be reviewed continuously, because a tool's function or configuration can affect what data is processed and sent externally.

## Legal issue

The central legal issue was whether Kry had implemented appropriate technical and organisational measures under Article 32 GDPR for the processing connected to Meta Pixel.

IMY assessed whether Kry had sufficient control, procedures, testing, review, and ability to detect unintended changes in personal data processing when using tracking technology.

The case is relevant to GDPR security, Article 32, third-party tracking, unintended transfers, hashed contact information, data protection by design, and personal data breach handling.

It also raises the question of how organisations should control tracking tools when the processing takes place in a healthcare-related context.

## Decision or outcome

IMY found that Kry had processed personal data in breach of Article 32(1) GDPR.

IMY assessed that Kry had not implemented all appropriate technical and organisational measures in relation to the risks of the processing.

IMY considered the infringement minor overall and issued a reprimand under Article 58(2)(b) GDPR instead of imposing an administrative fine.

The decision shows that a case can involve a GDPR infringement without necessarily resulting in a financial penalty.

It also shows that reprimands can still be important enforcement outcomes, especially when they clarify what an organisation should have controlled or documented better.

IMY ansåg att Kry inte hade infört alla lämpliga tekniska och organisatoriska åtgärder i förhållande till riskerna med behandlingen.

IMY bedömde överträdelsen som mindre allvarlig i sin helhet och utfärdade en reprimand enligt artikel 58.2 b i GDPR i stället för att besluta om en administrativ sanktionsavgift.

Beslutet visar att ett ärende kan innebära en GDPR-överträdelse utan att det nödvändigtvis leder till en ekonomisk sanktionsavgift.

Det visar också att reprimander fortfarande kan vara viktiga tillsynsutfall, särskilt när de tydliggör vad en organisation borde ha kontrollerat eller dokumenterat bättre.

## Fine or cost

Administrative fine or outcome:

Kry International AB: Reprimand, no administrative fine in this decision.

CyberLex should not present this outcome as a prediction for other Meta Pixel cases.

This case is important because it shows that Meta Pixel and similar tracking tools can create GDPR risk even when data is hashed and even when the organisation did not intend to transfer end-user data.

The outcome also shows that similar technology can lead to different GDPR consequences depending on the context, risk level, affected data, safeguards, mitigation, and the authority's final assessment.

## Why it matters for CyberLex

This case is useful because it gives a more nuanced Meta Pixel example than cases with large fines.

It shows that GDPR consequences are case-specific.

Similar tracking technology can lead to different outcomes depending on the type of data, context, scope, safeguards, mitigation, whether data was public, whether data was sensitive, the organisation's controls, and the authority's overall assessment.

The case is also important because it shows that hashed contact information may still matter under GDPR.

A technical safeguard such as hashing does not automatically remove all privacy risk if the data can still relate to identifiable individuals or be used for matching.

The case can be used to explain why organisations should review third-party scripts before deployment, test them after changes, monitor how they behave, and document decisions about tracking technology.

It is relevant for questions about tracking tools, healthcare contexts, third-party analytics, Article 32 security measures, data protection by design, technical configuration, and unintended personal data transfers.

## Learning note

This case shows that hashed contact information can still create GDPR risk when it is transferred through tracking technology.

It also shows that outcomes can differ between cases, because IMY issued a reprimand instead of an administrative fine.

The case is a useful reminder that tracking tools should be treated as active processing components, not harmless website decorations.

Organisations should understand what data the tool collects, when the tool sends data to a third party, whether matching functions are enabled, and whether the processing is appropriate for the context.

This is especially important in healthcare-related environments, where users may reasonably expect a high level of confidentiality and care.
