# Sportadmin Security Breach

## Case type

Authority decision / GDPR security measures / personal data breach / cyber attack.

## Jurisdiction

Sweden.

## Year

2026.

## Authority or court

Integritetsskyddsmyndigheten (IMY), the Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection.

## Topic

Sportadmin, IT attack, personal data breach, Article 32 GDPR, security measures, children and young people, sensitive data, Darknet publication.

## Short summary

IMY issued an administrative fine against Sportadmin after an IT attack where a large volume of personal data was accessed and later published on the Darknet.

The incident affected more than 2.1 million people, making it one of the larger personal data breach examples in this case library.

The leaked data mainly concerned children and young people connected to sports clubs and associations.

The information included names, contact details, personal identity numbers, sports and club connection, and in some cases sensitive health data and protected identity information.

The case is especially serious because the affected group included children and young people, who can be more vulnerable when personal data is exposed.

It is also serious because publication on the Darknet can increase the risk of misuse, identity-related harm, harassment, fraud, or further spread of the data.

IMY found that Sportadmin had not maintained an appropriate level of security for the personal data it processed.

The case shows that a company can be the victim of an attack and still be assessed under GDPR for whether its security measures were appropriate before and during the incident.

It also shows that Article 32 GDPR is not only about having some security controls, but about having controls that match the risk, scale, type of data, and affected individuals.

The case is useful as a strong example of how weak or insufficient security measures can turn a cyberattack into a large-scale GDPR enforcement matter.

## What happened

Sportadmin operated a service used by sports clubs and associations.

In January 2025, an attacker gained access to personal data processed in the service.

The attacker obtained data relating to more than 2.1 million individuals and later published the data on the Darknet.

The incident involved a large amount of data about children and young people.

Some of the leaked information was especially sensitive because it included health-related data and information about persons with protected identity.

The leaked data also included information connected to sports and club memberships, which can reveal details about a person's activities, location, routines, and social context.

Because the service was used by many associations, the incident affected a broad group of people rather than only one isolated organization.

The publication of the data on the Darknet made the breach more serious because the information could be copied, redistributed, searched, or used by unknown third parties.

The case is therefore relevant both as a cybersecurity incident and as a GDPR case about large-scale personal data protection.

## Legal issue

The central legal issue was whether Sportadmin had implemented appropriate technical and organisational security measures under Article 32 GDPR.

IMY assessed whether Sportadmin had a security level appropriate to the risk, considering the scale of the processing, the type of personal data, the affected individuals, and the risk connected to known weaknesses and possible attacks.

The case is relevant to access control, intrusion protection, monitoring, vulnerability management, incident detection, data protection by design, and risk-based security.

It is also relevant because the processing involved children and young people, and in some cases sensitive personal data and protected identity information.

## Decision or outcome

IMY found that Sportadmin had processed personal data in breach of Article 32(1) GDPR.

IMY stated that Sportadmin had not implemented appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure a suitable level of security before and at the time of the personal data breach.

IMY issued an administrative fine of SEK 6 million against Sportadmin i Skandinavien AB.

The decision shows that the existence of a cyberattack does not remove the organization’s duty to have risk-based security measures.

It also shows that large-scale processing, children’s data, sensitive data, and Darknet publication can make the consequences of weak security much more serious.

CyberLex should not present this amount as a fine prediction for other cases.

The amount is a historical example from one IMY decision.

Fine levels depend on the facts of each case, including security measures, type of data, number of affected people, organisational conduct, risk level, mitigation, and other GDPR factors.

Factors that may matter in similar cases include whether children were affected, whether sensitive data was involved, whether data was published externally, what security measures existed, how quickly the incident was detected, and how the organization handled the breach.

## Why it matters for CyberLex

This case is highly relevant because it connects cybersecurity, personal data breaches, Article 32 GDPR security measures, and real-world harm.

It shows that a cyberattack does not automatically mean the organization is responsible for the attack itself.

However, the organization may still be assessed on whether it had appropriate security measures in place before and during the incident.

The case is also important because it involved children and young people, which increases the need for careful risk assessment and strong safeguards.

A large-scale breach can create serious risks when personal identity numbers, contact details, sports affiliation, health-related information, or protected identity information are exposed.

The Darknet publication makes the case especially useful when explaining why incident response must include risk assessment, communication, containment, evidence preservation, and follow-up measures.

## Learning note

This case shows how weak security measures can lead to large-scale personal data exposure after a cyberattack.

It is especially important because the incident affected children and young people and included sensitive information.

The case also shows that GDPR security is risk-based. The more sensitive the data, the larger the scale, and the more vulnerable the affected individuals are, the stronger the expected security measures become.

Organizations that process large volumes of personal data should have strong access controls, monitoring, vulnerability management, incident detection, backup routines, logging, and documented security governance.

The obvious lesson, which humanity keeps rediscovering at great expense, is that “we were attacked” is not the same as “we had appropriate security.”

## Swedish learning note

Fallet visar hur svaga säkerhetsåtgärder kan leda till storskalig exponering av personuppgifter efter en cyberattack.

Det är särskilt viktigt eftersom incidenten berörde barn och unga och omfattade känsliga uppgifter.

Fallet visar också att GDPR-säkerhet är riskbaserad. Ju känsligare uppgifterna är, ju större behandlingen är och ju mer sårbara de berörda personerna är, desto starkare säkerhetsåtgärder förväntas.

Organisationer som behandlar stora mängder personuppgifter bör ha stark åtkomstkontroll, övervakning, sårbarhetshantering, incidentdetektering, säkerhetskopiering, loggning och dokumenterad säkerhetsstyrning.

Den uppenbara lärdomen, som mänskligheten ständigt återupptäcker till hög kostnad, är att “vi blev attackerade” inte är samma sak som “vi hade lämplig säkerhet.”
